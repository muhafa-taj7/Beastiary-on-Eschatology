Kitāb al-Asrār · Entry the Second

On the Celestial Steed

فِي ذِكْرِ الْبُرَاقِ مَرْكَبِ الْأَنْبِيَاءِ

Al-Burāq الْبُرَاق "The Lightning-One" · Conveyance of the Prophets in the Night Journey
✦

Essential Attributes

Classification Celestial Creature of the Heavenly Realms (مَخْلُوقٌ سَمَاوِيّ)
Ḥadīth Locus Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim, Kitāb al-Īmān; Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī, Kitāb Manāqib al-Anṣār
Nature Luminous; swifter than lightning; of intermediate size between donkey and mule
Function Divine conveyance for prophets; vehicle of the Isrāʾ and Miʿrāj
Arabic Name الْبُرَاق

Scriptural Origins

سُبْحَانَ الَّذِي أَسْرَىٰ بِعَبْدِهِ لَيْلًا مِّنَ الْمَسْجِدِ الْحَرَامِ إِلَى الْمَسْجِدِ الْأَقْصَى الَّذِي بَارَكْنَا حَوْلَهُ

"Exalted is He who transported His servant by night from the Sacred Mosque to the Furthest Mosque, whose surroundings We have blessed…"

— Sūrat al-Isrāʾ (The Night Journey), 17:1

While the Qurʾān records the divine event of the Night Journey, it is in the prophetic narrations that the Burāq finds its fullest description. The foundational account is that preserved by Anas ibn Mālik (may God be pleased with him) in Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim, wherein the Messenger ﷺ recounts how جِبْرِيل — the archangel Gabriel — brought to him the Burāq, and the creature shied from the Prophet ﷺ until Gabriel reproached it, saying: "Is it from Muhammad that you hold back? For by God, no one more honoured than he has ever ridden you."

From the Ḥadīth of Mālik ibn Ṣaʿṣaʿa (Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī, no. 3887) The Prophet ﷺ said: "Then a white animal was brought to me, smaller than a mule and larger than a donkey…" Its stride reaches as far as the eye can see. Gabriel placed me upon it, and it set off with me until we reached the lowest heaven…"
❧

Physical Description

The name al-Burāq derives from the root ب ر ق (barq) — "lightning" — indicating both its speed and its luminous, gleaming quality.

In the authenticated traditions, the Burāq is described as white (أَبْيَض) and luminous, its very body emanating a radiance appropriate to a creature of the celestial realms. It is larger than a donkey yet smaller than a mule — a size suited to carry a prophet in dignity. Its most remarkable characteristic is its stride: each step falls at the furthest limit of the rider's sight, covering in one bound what no earthly mount could traverse in a day's journey.

Later traditions and the pictorial traditions of the Persian miniature schools — particularly those of the Timurid and Safavid ateliers — elaborated the Burāq's form considerably, depicting it with a human face, the body of a horse, the wings of an eagle or peacock, and a tail adorned with peacock feathers. Scholars such as al-Qasṭallānī note these elaborations while affirming that the core authenticated description mentions only its whiteness, its intermediate size, and the miracle of its stride. God knows best the full reality of His creatures.

Described Features

  • White, gleaming body
  • Larger than donkey, smaller than mule
  • Stride reaching the horizon
  • Brought by the archangel Gabriel
  • Capable of celestial travel
  • Previously ridden by earlier prophets
  • Possessed of awareness and temperament
  • Tethered at the Furthest Mosque (al-Aqṣā)

The Isrāʾ: Journey of the Night

Upon the Burāq, the Prophet Muḥammad ﷺ was carried in a single night from the Masjid al-Ḥarām in Makkah to the Masjid al-Aqṣā in Jerusalem — the first stage of the journey known as the الْإِسْرَاء (Isrāʾ, the Night Journey). There, the Prophet ﷺ led all the prophets in prayer, and the Burāq was tethered at the ring by the gate of the mosque. Thereafter commenced the الْمِعْرَاج (Miʿrāj, the Ascension) through the seven heavens — though scholars differ as to whether the Burāq accompanied the Prophet ﷺ beyond Jerusalem or whether Gabriel alone guided the ascent thereafter.

ثُمَّ أُتِيتُ بِدَابَّةٍ أَبْيَضَ يُقَالُ لَهُ الْبُرَاقُ فَوْقَ الْحِمَارِ وَدُونَ الْبَغْلِ يَضَعُ خَطْوَهُ عِنْدَ أَقْصَى طَرْفِهِ

"Then I was brought a white animal called the Burāq — larger than a donkey yet smaller than a mule — which places its step at the furthest limit of its sight."

— Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim, Kitāb al-Īmān, no. 162

Thus concludes the second entry of the Bestiary of Signs.
May God's blessings and peace be upon the one who rode the Burāq.
