A Book of Wonders · Entry the Third

Al-Masīḥ al-Dajjāl

الْمَسِيحُ الدَّجَّال

The Deceiving Messiah الْمَسِيحُ الدَّجَّال The First & Greatest Sign · The One-Eyed Deceiver · A Very Great Fitnah
✦

Physical Description

Al-Dajjāl is short in stature, with curly and thick hair. His right eye is blind — described as a floating grape — while his left eye has skin over it. He is white in complexion, with a wide forehead. He has no children. Most distinctively, the word Kāfir (كَافِر) — meaning disbeliever — is written between his eyes, and it is said that only the believers will be able to read it. These details are drawn from ʿArīfī, The End of the World, pp. 274–275.

Lore & Significance

The Dajjāl is a one-eyed human or monstrous figure who leads a kingdom of terror and attempts to seduce believers into idolatry. He is the first and greatest sign and represents a very great فِتْنَة (fitnah — tribulation) for the world. He will emerge from a land in the east called Khorasan, between Syria and Iraq, after three years of progressive famine. In the first year, God will command the sky to withhold a third of its rain; in the second, two thirds; and in the third, all rain will cease. He will then offer food — a temptation for the believers. (ʿArīfī, The End of the World, pp. 273, 275–276.)

He will seem to possess god-like powers. He will appear to "resurrect" someone's mother and father from the dead by commanding two devils to appear in their shape, deceiving those who witness it. He will be used to control people, and is made out to be an evil person. Fear of him is countered by acts of worship — for example, memorizing the first ten verses of Sūrat al-Kahf. He is unable to enter Makkah or Madīna. The Dajjāl will ultimately be slain by ʿĪsā ibn Maryam (Jesus, son of Mary, peace be upon him).

The Dajjāl is a metaphor for the temptations of life and its temporariness. What he offers looks lavish but will eventually fade. Islam is the only truth — deception is everywhere. Do not believe everyone; maintain doubt and discernment. Being educated about him is the protection against him, so that one never ends up in a situation under his influence. His food, his false miracles, and his apparent power all represent the seductive yet hollow nature of worldly temptation. Asceticism and trust in God — who will provide — is the ideological counter.
