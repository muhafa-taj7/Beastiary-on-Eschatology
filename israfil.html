A Book of Wonders · Entry the Eighth

Isrāfīl

إِسْرَافِيل

Isrāfīl — The Angel of the Trumpet إِسْرَافِيل The Blower of the Ṣūr · Herald of the Hour · Angel of the Resurrection
✦

Physical Description

Isrāfīl is an archangel — one of the greatest of God's angelic creation — whose specific function is to blow the الصُّور (al-Ṣūr), the great trumpet, at the command of God. He is depicted in Islamic manuscript tradition as a mighty and radiant winged figure, poised at all times with the trumpet at his lips, awaiting the divine command to blow. He has been in this state of readiness since his creation.

Lore & Significance

Isrāfīl is the angel responsible for blowing the trumpet that signals the arrival of the Hour and the resurrection of all souls. The blowing of the trumpet occurs in stages: the first blast causes all creatures to fall unconscious or die; the second raises them all to stand before God for the Final Judgment. Between the two blasts, God alone remains — for He is the Ever-Living, the Self-Sustaining, from whom all existence flows and to whom it all returns.

Isrāfīl stands at the end of the sequence of the major signs. After the Sufyānī, the Mahdī, the Dajjāl, the descent of ʿĪsā, the release of Yaʾjūj wa-Maʾjūj, and the emergence of al-Dābba — after all these have come to pass and the age of signs has closed — it is Isrāfīl who sounds the ultimate signal, announcing the definitive end of this world and the opening of the next.

The Qurʾān refers to the blowing of the trumpet in multiple verses, including Sūrat al-Zumar (39:68): "And the trumpet will be blown, and whoever is in the heavens and whoever is on the earth will fall dead, except whom Allah wills. Then it will be blown again, and at once they will be standing, looking on."

Isrāfīl represents the absolute sovereignty and finality of God's command. He has waited since his creation for the moment of the Blow — poised, ready, patient — as a reminder that the Hour is always near, even when it seems distant. His role closes the bestiary not with a creature of earthly terror but with a celestial herald, a messenger of transition: from this world to the next, from the temporary to the eternal. The themes of this book — martyrdom, narrative, eschatology, motive and intent, exegesis — all converge at the sound of his trumpet.
