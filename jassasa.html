A Book of Wonders · Entry the Fourth

Al-Jassāsa

الْجَسَّاسَة

The Spy الْجَسَّاسَة The Hairy Beast of the Island · Keeper of the Dajjāl's Secret
✦

Physical Description

Al-Jassāsa is a beast with a great deal of hair — so much that those who encounter it cannot distinguish its face from its back. It is described as a female monster of fabulous and fearsome aspect, associated with a remote island far out at sea. Those who meet it cannot determine its gender or form with certainty, so overwhelming is its covering of hair.

Lore & Significance

From the Ḥadīth of Fāṭima bint Qays · Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim ʿĀmir ibn Sharāḥīl al-Shaʿbī narrated that he asked Fāṭima bint Qays, the sister of al-Daḥḥāk ibn Qays — one of the earliest Muhājir women — to relate a hadith she had heard directly from the Messenger of Allah ﷺ. She said: "I heard the voice of the caller of the Messenger of Allah ﷺ saying: 'Al-ṣalātu jāmiʿah' (prayer is about to begin), so I went out to the mosque and prayed with the Messenger of Allah ﷺ. When he had finished his prayer, he sat on the minbar and he was smiling. He said: 'Let each person stay in the place where he just prayed.' Then he said: 'Do you know why I called you together?' They said: 'Allah and His Messenger know best.' He said: 'By Allah, I did not call you together for something good or for some alarming news. I have called you together because Tamīm al-Dārī was a Christian and he came and swore allegiance and became Muslim, and told me something which agrees with what I was telling you about the Dajjāl.'"

Tamīm al-Dārī recounted that he sailed in a ship with thirty men of Lakhm and Judhām, and they were tossed by the waves of the sea for a month. They came to an island at sunset, sat in a small rowing-boat, and landed. They were met by a beast with a great deal of hair, and they could not distinguish its face from its back because it was so hairy. They said: "Woe to you — what are you?" It said: "I am al-Jassāsa." She then directed them toward a man chained in a monastery on the island — who was the Dajjāl, waiting for his appointed time of release.

Al-Jassāsa's role is thus that of a guardian and spy — she watches over the island, collects information about the world for the Dajjāl, and directs strangers toward him. The archangels Mīkāʾīl and Jibrīl will serve as protectors during the Dajjāl's reign: Mīkāʾīl acts as a shield to prevent the Dajjāl from entering Mecca, and Jibrīl protects Medina. (Cuciniello, "Investigating the Dajjāl," p. 131.)

Al-Jassāsa is among the most mysterious figures of the eschatological tradition. Her story reaches us through the direct testimony of a Companion of the Prophet ﷺ, which the Prophet ﷺ himself confirmed publicly on the minbar — lending it unusual narrative authority. She represents the hidden and the liminal: the creature that belongs to neither world fully, existing in the space between the present age and the coming catastrophe.
