A Book of Wonders · Entry the Second

The Mahdī

الْمَهْدِيّ

Al-Mahdī — The Rightly Guided One الْمَهْدِيّ Eschatological Savior · Descendant of the Prophet ﷺ · Figure of Justice
✦

Physical Description

The Mahdī is described as having a high forehead and a prominent nose. He is Muḥammad ibn ʿAbd Allāh al-Ḥasanī al-ʿAlawī — a descendant of the Prophet ﷺ through Fāṭima (may God be pleased with her) by way of al-Ḥasan. His name is the same as the Prophet's name, and his father's name is the same as the Prophet's father's name.

Lore & Significance

The Mahdī is an eschatological savior who appears to fill the world with justice and to unite the Muslim community before the return of Jesus. The Prophet ﷺ said: "Even if only one day was left of this world, Allah would make that day long so that He could send a man who is of me or of my family, whose name is the same as my name and whose father's name is the same as my father's name." This tradition was recorded by al-Tirmidhī and Abū Dāwūd, and classed as Ṣaḥīḥ by Shaykh al-Islām Ibn Taymiyya in Minhāj al-Sunna, 4/211.

He will appear during a time of oppression and injustice, and amid fighting among the sons of caliphs. He will be a leader who gathers the people and guides them in battles. He will rule for seven years. The Mahdī will emerge after a period of great disorder, and will be a righteous ruler who fills the world with justice. He is believed in the Shia tradition to be the Twelfth Imam.

As to why the Mahdī comes from the line of al-Ḥasan rather than al-Ḥusayn: al-Ḥasan (may God be pleased with him) was appointed caliph after the martyrdom of ʿAlī (may God be pleased with him). He ruled for six months and then gave up his rule to Muʿāwiya in order to unite the Muslims — and so he was rewarded for this sacrifice by the Mahdī being from his descendants. As Ibn al-Qayyim writes in Al-Manār al-Munīf (pg. 151): sacrifice brings blessings beyond this world.

He is a figure of the savior — and sacrifice is important, with blessings extending outside of this world. The Mahdī represents the hope that even in the darkest of times, God will send a restorer of justice. His appearance is tied to the worst conditions of oppression, and his emergence is a sign of divine mercy toward the believers.
