The Sufyānī

السُّفْيَانِيّ

Al-Sufyānī السُّفْيَانِيّ Descendant of Abū Sufyān · The Great Antagonist of the End Times
✦

Physical Description

The Sufyānī is described as having a huge head, with scars of smallpox upon his face, and a white spot in one eye. Other reports further characterize him as cross-eyed, jaundiced in complexion, and having weak forearms and calves. He is of the progeny of Abū Sufyān, specifically through the line of Khālid ibn Yazīd ibn Muʿāwiya, and he emerges from a location to the west of Syria known as the Dry Valley (وَادِي الْيَابِس).

Lore & Significance

The Sufyānī is a central antagonist of the end times whose appearance marks a critical turning point in the sequence of major tribulations. He emerges during a time of extreme internal division within the Muslim community, and his rise occurs amidst a chaotic conflict involving three primary factions. He is victorious over the Marwānids and other rivals in Syria before turning his attention to the easterners and the ʿAbbāsids. He is predicted to rule for a period roughly equivalent to the length of a woman's pregnancy — nine months — though some traditions suggest variations such as three and a half years.

He is characterized as the "evilest of those who have reigned," specifically targeting the learned (عُلَمَاء) and people of merit. He will besiege Damascus and defeat the Turks and Byzantines in a massive battle at Carchemish (قِرْقِيسِيَاء). Upon entering al-Kūfa, he treats its people horribly, killing up to sixty thousand and taking offspring and women captive. He is associated with the destruction of Baghdad, where he slaughters six of the ʿAbbāsid family. He sends an army to Medina that kills four hundred men and crucifies a pair of siblings, Muḥammad and Fāṭima, at the door of the mosque. He is infamously noted for slitting the bellies of pregnant women.

The Sufyānī's actions serve as the final catalyst for the appearance of the Mahdī. He sends an expedition from Kūfa toward Mecca to capture the Mahdī — this army will camp in a wasteland (الْبَيْدَاء), where they will be swallowed up by the earth. This event is described as the definitive sign of the Mahdī's legitimacy. Following the swallowing of his army, the Sufyānī initially offers an oath of allegiance to the Mahdī. However, the tribe of Kalb — his maternal uncles — condemn him for giving up his power and persuade him to retract his oath, leading to a final battle in which the Mahdī is victorious. The Sufyānī is ultimately captured and slaughtered, with different accounts placing his death at the Gate of Mercy, the Jayrūn Gate, or the flagstones of Jerusalem.

Muslims win. The Sufyānī represents the ultimate antagonist who, despite his terrifying power and atrocities, is ultimately brought down through divine will. His story is a source of support and reassurance for believers — showing that even the most fearsome oppression is temporary and that justice will prevail. His figure is questionable in the hadith tradition, sourced primarily from The Book of Tribulations of Nuʿaym ibn Ḥammād al-Marwazī (pg. 142).
