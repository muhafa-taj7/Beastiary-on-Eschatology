A Book of Wonders · Entry the Sixth

Yaʾjūj wa-Maʾjūj

يَأْجُوجُ وَمَأْجُوجُ

Gog and Magog يَأْجُوجُ وَمَأْجُوجُ The Third Sign · Savage Peoples Behind the Wall · The Corrupters of the Earth
✦

Physical Description

Yaʾjūj wa-Maʾjūj are human in nature — they are a nation, with their own societies and communities. They are described as having broad faces, small eyes, reddish-black hair, and faces like hammered shields. They are a very mischievous nation. (ʿArīfī, The End of the World, p. 366.)

Lore & Significance

Gog and Magog are the third sign of the end times. They were contained behind a great wall of steel built by Dhū al-Qarnayn, a mighty king, who was asked to build it in order to block them in. They dig at the wall constantly, but God replenishes it whenever they are close to breaking through — until the appointed time comes, when God gives them the ability to say "inshā Allāh" and they break through. (ʿArīfī, The End of the World, p. 370.) This detail is significant: even those who are not Muslim are subject to the will of God — only Allah can give permission to do things.

After the death of the Dajjāl, they are released and overrun the earth. ʿĪsā (peace be upon him) will be with the believers, who suffer great hunger and exhaustion from hiding from them. Yaʾjūj wa-Maʾjūj kill everyone on earth except those in hiding, and then turn to the sky and shoot their arrows upward — which come back stained with blood. They believe they have killed those in heaven. (ʿArīfī, The End of the World, pp. 380–381.) God will then send a worm that kills them all; birds will clean them up, rain will wash the earth, and the world will be rid of them. After their destruction, the world will be peaceful — no fighting or war. (ʿArīfī, The End of the World, p. 383.)

As for the afterlife: from every thousand people who enter hell, nine hundred and ninety-nine will be from Yaʾjūj wa-Maʾjūj, and one from the rest of mankind. (ʿArīfī, The End of the World, pp. 366–369.)

Their story is meant to represent not giving up. Even ʿĪsā (peace be upon him) will have to hide — showing that sometimes even the strongest must protect themselves — but he will not give up, because Allah will always protect the believers. The arrogance of Yaʾjūj wa-Maʾjūj in shooting at heaven is the ultimate expression of human hubris: no one can kill God and those in heaven except through Allah's command. They think they will have won — but they have ultimately failed. Their narrative also provides a sense of support and reassurance to believers: from every thousand people in hell, nine hundred and ninety-nine are from this people — showing that the scale of divine judgment falls on the oppressors, not the faithful.
